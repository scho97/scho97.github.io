Research Interest

My primary research interests lie in computational cognitive neuroscience. I am broadly interested in the application of the mathematical modeling, neuroimaging, electrophysiology, and machine learning for understanding higher cognitive abilities and their dysfunctions in the human brain. My academic curiosities are epistemological in their origin and can be summarized as below:

  • Human knowledge and reason
  • The way in which these knowledge are conceptualized and communicated
  • The neural representations of truth, logic (whether empirical or propositional), and justified belief
  • The mechanisms by which all these complexities can go astray

In general, these topics demand an answer to an inquiry of how the brain processes and computes information from the external environment and builds complexities onto it. Some of the specific questions I look forward to answer under such motivation are specified below in two levels of study.

At systems level

At the systems level, I am interested in the circuit architecture and the underlying computation behind our cognitive ability, as well as the malfunctioning of neural circuits inherent to neuropsychiatric disorders.

  1. What are the biological and artificial neural networks that can most accurately explain the dynamical neuronal states?
  2. How are information transmitted and processed within a single circuit or across multi-scale networks?
  3. How are these structural and functional components reflected in the behaviors?

At cognitive level

At the cognitive level, I am interested in abstract (e.g., logical, mathematical) cognition and decision making.

  1. How are symbolic languages and logical properties (e.g., negation, conditionality) encoded and represented in the brain?
  2. How do abilities like causal inference, induction, pattern and rule recognition, and categorization occur?
  3. How are decisions planned and strategized under morally or materially conflicting situations?
