Research Interest

My academic aspiration originates from epistemological concerns. Hence, inquiries I have about the nature include: (1) how we attain knowledge and reason, (2) what the biological structures of the truth (whether empirical or propositional) and justified belief are, and (3) how our knowledge can be conceptualized (e.g. visually) and communicated.
These questions, however, may appear too abstract and philosophical to many scientists. Yet, I believe neuroscience is the means to which I can formulate such questions scientifically tangible and answer them, by studying how brain processes and computes information from the external environment and builds complexities onto it. My primary research interests lie in theoretical and computational neuroscience, with secondary but strong predilection in human electrophysiology and neuroimaging.
My research interests can be specified into two levels of investigation.

At systems level

  • What are the circuit architecture and its underlying computation behind our cognitive ability?

At cognitive level

Bio

I am a postgraduate research intern in Laboratory of Computational, Cognitive & Systems Neuroscience at Korea Institute of Science and Technology. My current work focuses on the question of how neural oscillations may be translated into animal behaviors and whether the oscillatory bursting phenomenon reliably represents emergence of transient functional networks (i.e. long-range neural communications) when particular behaviors take place. Previously, I studied neuroscience and philosophy at the University of Chicago where I worked with Professor Wim van Drongelen on nonlinear dynamical systems of focal epileptic seizures. Outside of neuroscience, I invested most of my times studying epistemology under the context of philosophy of logic, mathematics, and language.
