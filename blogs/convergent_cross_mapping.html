Convergent Cross Mapping Algorithm

Definition of Convergent Cross Mapping (CCM)

That correlation does not imply and is not a necessary and sufficient condition for causation is a well-established idea in science. Identifying causal relationships in time series is a challenging task, especially if the underlying complex systems are nonlinear and temporally lagged. Along with the Granger causality (GC) and phase slope index, one of the methods that can address such causality in complex systems is called convergent cross mapping (CCM).

CCM is a statistical method for inferring causality based on nonlinear state space reconstruction [1]. Simply, consider two time-series variables X and Y. In dynamical systems theory, if X and Y are from same dynamic system, then these two time-series variables are causally related and share a common attractor manifold M [1]. By characterizing correspondence between the shadow manifolds that are reconstructed from lagged coordinates of each time series, nonlinear interactions between time series X and Y can be defined [2]. That is, because the state of one variable can be identified (i.e. recovered) by that of another, CCM uses the estimate value of Y from the shadow manifold X and the estimate value of X from the shadow manifold Y to quantify the causal relationship between the two variables.

Takens' Theorem (1981)

Takens' theorem, also known as the delayed embedding theorem, states that the common attractor manifold of a dynamical system can be reconstructed from a single observation variable. For example, given an observation function \(X(t)\) and its lagged-coordinate functions \(X(t-\tau), ..., X(t-n\tau)\), a shadow manifold of of \(X\), denoted here as \(M_X\), can be reconstructed.
